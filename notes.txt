Monkeys are primates that belong to two main groups: Old World monkeys (found in Africa and Asia) and New World monkeys (found in the Americas).
There are over 260 known species of monkeys, making them one of the most diverse groups of mammals on Earth.
Monkeys are highly social animals and typically live in groups called troops, which can range from a few individuals to hundreds.
Unlike apes, most monkeys have tails. Some New World monkeys, like howler and spider monkeys, have prehensile tails that can grip branches like a fifth limb.
The mandrill is the world's largest monkey. Males can weigh up to 36 kg and are famous for their colourful blue and red facial markings.
Pygmy marmosets are the world's smallest monkeys, weighing as little as 100 grams — roughly the weight of a small apple.
Howler monkeys are among the loudest animals on Earth. Their calls can be heard up to 5 kilometres away through dense rainforest.
Monkeys communicate through a combination of vocalisations, facial expressions, and body language. Some species have been observed using rudimentary tools in the wild.
Japanese macaques (snow monkeys) are the northernmost-living non-human primates. They are famously known for bathing in hot springs during winter in Japan.
Many monkey species are endangered due to habitat destruction, hunting, and the illegal pet trade. Deforestation is the single biggest threat to wild monkey populations worldwide.
